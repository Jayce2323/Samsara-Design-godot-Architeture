											*Muscle Gain & Loss System*

				MUSCLE GAIN
	Triggered by: Caloric surplus + strenuous activity + high carry weight
	
	gain_modifier = (avg_daily_heavy_activity_kcal / 1000.0) + (avg_daily_carry_weight_kg / carry_capacity)
	
	Hard cap: MUSCLE_CREATION_CAP_KCAL = 3500 kcal per day max muscle gain
	
	Logic:  Character must have surplus calories AND be performing heavy activity/carrying weight. 
	The surplus is converted to muscle up to the daily cap.
	Without activity stimulus, surplus goes to fat instead.

				MUSCLE LOSS
	Triggered by: Activity imbalance (sedentary > heavy) + moderate activity presence
	
	muscle_loss_factor = [avg_weekly_sedentary_minutes - avg_weekly_heavy_activity_minutes] Ã— [avg_weekly_moderate_activity_minutes Ã— 0.1]
	
	Logic: If character spends more time sedentary than doing heavy work, muscles atrophy.
	The presence of moderate activity slows atrophy (0.1 multiplier reduces loss).
	Pure sedentary (zero moderate activity) = maximum loss.
	Balanced or heavy-focused weeks = no loss or potential gain. 

				CALORIC CONVERSION
	Muscle mass stored as:  current_converted_value_ccv_kcal (3500 kcal = 1 kg muscle)
	Fat mass stored as: current_reserve_value_crv_kcal (7700 kcal = 1 kg fat)
	
	Deficit burn ratio: 3: 1 (Muscle: Fat) - muscles burned 3x faster than fat when in deficit

	Daily: Calculate activity stimulus for muscle gain potential
	Weekly: Calculate activity pattern for muscle loss/atrophy

Macronutrient Absorption Rates [Digestion]

Carbohydrates (single source): Approximately 60 grams per hour (~240 kcal/hr) using a single transport mechanism (e.g., glucose only).
Carbohydrates (multiple sources): Up to 90-105 grams per hour (~360-420 kcal/hr) when combining multiple transportable carbohydrates (e.g., a glucose and fructose mixture at a 2:1 ratio).
Protein: The rate is highly dependent on the type of protein:
Fast-digesting protein [Lean protien](e.g., whey): Roughly 8-10 grams per hour (~32-40 kcal/hr).
Slow-digesting protein [Fatty protien](e.g., cooked egg): About 3 grams per hour (~12 kcal/hr).
Fat: Fat absorption is complex and generally slower than carbohydrate or protein absorption per hour during exercise, and it slows gastric emptying. While fat provides 9 kcal per gram, a high-fat meal takes much longer to process overall. 
The body is highly efficient at absorbing nearly all fat consumed over the full digestive cycle, but the hourly rate during physical exertion is lower than for simple carbs. *Base starting value About 1 gram per hour (~9 kcal/hr)

								Task Completion Time Calculation - " calculate - Skill effect on task"		

% 1. TOTAL OBSERVED TIME (The Raw Baseline)
T_{obs} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i

% 2. NORMAL TIME (Adjusting for Subject Skill/Performance)
T_n = T_{obs} \times \left( \frac{R}{100} \right)

% 3. STANDARD TIME (The Final Practical Estimate)
T_s = T_n \times (1 + A)

% WHERE:
% s_i = Time per individual step
% n   = Total number of steps (Task Complexity)

% R   = Performance Rating / Skill Factor (e.g., 100 = Standard)
% A   = Allowance Factor for fatigue/delays (e.g., 0.15 = 15%)



[Formula explanation: -The Estimation FormulaÂ The total estimated time is the sum of individual step durations, modified by a skill-based performance factor:Â 
			 	(T= [sum {i=1}^{n}s_{i}] x [{1}{S})\(s_{i})-> (1\S)] 
				(Individual Step-Times): The average time estimated for each discrete step in the task.
				(n) (Task Complexity): The total number of steps in the sequence.
				(S) (Subject Skill): A rating factor where 1.0 represents the "standard" or average skill level.

Â 			- Refining Your EstimateÂ To account for uncertainty or "minimum average time" (the best-case scenario), use these industry-standard methods:Â 
				Three-Point Estimation (PERT): Combine your best-case (optimistic), worst-case (pessimistic), and most-likely times to find a realistic average:
				(Estimate)= [O+4M+P]\6) 
				(O) is the optimistic (min) time, (M) is the most likely, and (P) is the pessimistic.

			- Normal Time Calculation: Professionals often use a Time Study Rating Factor to convert observed average time into "Normal Time" by multiplying the average time by a performance rating (Skill).
				Time Study Rating Factor (or Performance Rating) is used to normalize the time a specific person took to perform a task compared to the time a "standard" worker would take.Â 
				The Rating Factor FormulaÂ The rating factor is typically expressed as a percentage, where 100% represents a "normal" or standard pace.Â 
				[Rating Factor]= [Observed Performance]\[Standard Performance] x 100)Â 
				Rating > 100: The subject is working faster than the standard pace.Rating < 100: 
				The subject is working slower than the standard pace.Â

			-	To calculate the final Standard Time (Ts), we combine the complexity of the task, the subject's skill (performance rating), and an allowance factor for human fatigue or unavoidable delays.Â 
				
				1: Calculate Total Observed TimeÂ Sum the individual times recorded for each step in the task sequence to find the total time actually taken by the subject.Â 
					(T(obs)=[sum {i=1}^{n}s_{i}]

					(T(n) = T(obs) x [R\100]
